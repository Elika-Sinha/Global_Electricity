Sustainable Development Goal 7

The United Nations General Assembly in 2015 set up 17 sustainable development goals to design a "blueprint to achieve a better and more sustainable future for all". According to a World Bank report the proportion of people worldwide with access to electricity and clean fuels has increased substantially over the past decade. In 2018, 90 percent of the world's population had access to electricity, up from 82 percent in 2008, and 63 percent had access to clean fuels and technology for cooking, up from 55 percent. Recognizing the need for access to electrivity for all; this assignment investigates the access to energy globally and zooms into region, country and city level.

World Access to Electricity

The chart below accounts for the total number of population with as well as without access to electricity. As of the year 2019, 6.91 billion people have access to electricity which has risen from 4.33 billion in 1990. However, there are still 760.89 million people without access to electricity in 2019- and this number has decreased from 1.63 billion in 1990.

Access to Electricity by Region

The statistics indicate the evident disparity between regions and subregions around the world, particularly empahsizing on the regions where inaccess surpasses access. In Africa Eastern and Southern, people without access to electricity accounted for 318.5 million in 1990, the absolute change to this number was only around 4 million in 2000 (with 314.14 million people without access in 2000). This number has starkly risen to 372 million people who DO NOT have access to electricity in the year 2019; in contrast to North America where over the last 30 years there has been no part of the populace without access to electricity.

Energy Use Per Capita

The graph indicates energy use capita by country in KWH per person. Evidently, the levels of energy use by the population is lesser in the global south in comparison to the northern neighbours. In 1960s North America's per capita energy use was 72,922 kWh which increased to 89,236 kWh in 1990s and has now decreased to 79,897 kWh (2019). In contrast, Nigeria's per capita use in 1990 was 2,138 kWh which increased to 2,726 kWh in 2019. With an arguably diverse population range, the use of per capita energy indicates an evident divide between the amount produced as well as consumed.

The UN explains: "Energy is central to nearly every major challenge and opportunity the world faces today. Be it for jobs, security, climate change, food production or increasing incomes, access to energy for all is essential. Transitioning the global economy towards clean and sustainable sources of energy is one of our greatest challenges in the coming decades. Sustainable energy is an opportunity it transforms lives, economies and the planet."

But perhaps accessibility to electricity posses a greater challenge today.
